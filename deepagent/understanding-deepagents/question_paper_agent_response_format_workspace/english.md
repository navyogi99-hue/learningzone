# English Language Questions & Explanations

### **Question 21 (Synonym)**
**Select the most appropriate synonym of the given word:**

**DILIGENT**

*   **A.** Idle
*   **B.** Industrious
*   **C.** Careless
*   **D.** Ignorant

**Correct Answer:** **B**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** The word **'Diligent'** means having or showing care and conscientiousness in one's work or duties; hardworking. **'Industrious'** is a direct synonym as it also means diligent, hard-working, and active.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **A. Idle** is an antonym; it means lazy, inactive, or not spent in useful work.
    *   **C. Careless** is incorrect; it means not giving sufficient attention or thought to avoiding harm or errors, which is the opposite of the careful nature of diligence.
    *   **D. Ignorant** is incorrect; it means lacking knowledge, information, or awareness, which is unrelated to being hardworking.

### **Question 22 (Antonym)**
**Select the most appropriate antonym of the given word:**

**HOSTILE**

*   **A.** Friendly
*   **B.** Aggressive
*   **C.** Unfavorable
*   **D.** Belligerent

**Correct Answer:** **A**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** The word **'Hostile'** means showing or feeling opposition or dislike; unfriendly or antagonistic. Therefore, **'Friendly'** is its direct and perfect antonym.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **B. Aggressive** is a related/synonymous term; it means ready or likely to attack or confront.
    *   **C. Unfavorable** is a near-synonym; it means expressing disapproval or showing opposition.
    *   **D. Belligerent** is a direct synonym; it means hostile, combative, and aggressive.

### **Question 23 (Spotting the Error)**
**Identify the segment in the sentence that contains a grammatical error:**

**Neither the teacher nor the students was present in the staff room when the principal arrived.**

*   **A.** Neither the teacher
*   **B.** nor the students was
*   **C.** present in the staff room
*   **D.** when the principal arrived

**Correct Answer:** **B**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** This question tests the grammatical rule of **Subject-Verb Agreement** with correlative conjunctions. When two subjects are connected by **"neither... nor"** or **"either... or"**, the verb must agree with the subject closest to it. Here, the closer subject is **"the students"**, which is plural. Therefore, the singular helping verb **"was"** must be replaced with the plural helping verb **"were"** to make the sentence grammatically correct.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **A. Neither the teacher** contains no errors as it correctly initiates the correlative structure.
    *   **C. present in the staff room** is grammatically correct as a prepositional/adjectival phrase.
    *   **D. when the principal arrived** is correct as it sets the temporal context using the correct simple past tense ("arrived"), matching the past tense of the main clause.

### **Question 24 (Fill in the Blanks - Prepositions)**
**Fill in the blank with the most appropriate preposition:**

**We must adapt ourselves ______ the changing circumstances of life.**

*   **A.** with
*   **B.** to
*   **C.** in
*   **D.** for

**Correct Answer:** **B**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** The verb **"adapt"** (especially as a reflexive verb, e.g., "adapt ourselves/oneself") is idiomatic and collocated with the preposition **"to"** when it means to adjust or modify oneself to suit new conditions, environments, or circumstances. Thus, **"to"** is the only grammatically correct preposition in this context.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **A. with**, **C. in**, and **D. for** do not form the standard, grammatically recognized collocation with "adapt" in this context. Using them would be incorrect English usage.

### **Question 25 (Fill in the Blanks - Subject-Verb Agreement / Tenses)**
**Fill in the blank with the most appropriate verb form:**

**A large number of candidates ______ registered for the upcoming bank examination.**

*   **A.** has
*   **B.** have
*   **C.** is
*   **D.** was

**Correct Answer:** **B**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** This question tests a standard rule of **Subject-Verb Agreement**. The noun phrase **"A large number of"** is followed by a plural countable noun ("candidates") and always takes a **plural verb**. To form the active voice in the present perfect tense with the past participle "registered," we must use the plural auxiliary verb **"have"**.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **A. has** is incorrect because it is a singular verb. (Note: "The number of" takes a singular verb, but "A number of" always takes a plural verb).
    *   **C. is** and **D. was** are incorrect because they are singular verbs and do not fit grammatically to make a logical active perfect tense or a meaningful passive construction here.

### **Question 26 (Idioms and Phrases)**
**Select the option that best expresses the meaning of the given idiom:**

**At the eleventh hour**

*   **A.** At the very last moment
*   **B.** One hour before midnight
*   **C.** At an early stage
*   **D.** At an inappropriate time

**Correct Answer:** **A**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** The idiom **"at the eleventh hour"** is a highly common English expression that means doing something at the last possible moment, right before a deadline or right before it is too late to make a change. Thus, **Option A** is the correct definition.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **B. One hour before midnight** is incorrect as it is a literal and physical interpretation of the hours of a clock, which is not what figurative idioms represent.
    *   **C. At an early stage** is incorrect because it expresses the opposite meaning.
    *   **D. At an inappropriate time** is incorrect because it does not capture the essential timing constraint of urgency and being right before a critical deadline.

### **Question 27 (One-Word Substitution)**
**Select the word which can be used as a one-word substitute for the given group of words:**

**A position or job that requires little or no work but provides a financial benefit.**

*   **A.** Sinecure
*   **B.** Honorary
*   **C.** Voluntary
*   **D.** Truant

**Correct Answer:** **A**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** **'Sinecure'** refers to a job or position that requires little or no active responsibility or work but yields a regular, profitable income or financial benefit.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **B. Honorary** refers to an office or title given as an honor, typically without payment or normal duties (e.g., an honorary degree).
    *   **C. Voluntary** refers to work or service done of one's own free will, usually without any financial compensation.
    *   **D. Truant** refers to a student who stays away from school without leave or explanation.

### **Question 28 (Correct Spelling)**
**Select the correctly spelled word from the given options:**

*   **A.** Accomodation
*   **B.** Accommodation
*   **C.** Acomodation
*   **D.** Accommadation

**Correct Answer:** **B**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:** The correct spelling is **"Accommodation"** (Option B). This is a classic spelling trap in competitive exams. The correct structure requires a double **'c'**, a double **'m'**, and an **'o'** in the middle syllable (**A-c-c-o-m-m-o-d-a-t-i-o-n**).
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **A. Accomodation** is misspelled because it contains only a single 'm'.
    *   **C. Acomodation** is misspelled because it contains a single 'c' and a single 'm'.
    *   **D. Accommadation** is misspelled because it has an 'a' instead of an 'o' in the middle syllable ("mad" instead of "mod").

### **Question 29 (Active/Passive Voice Conversion)**
**Select the option that represents the correct passive voice conversion of the given active sentence:**

**The chef prepared a delicious meal for the guests.**

*   **A.** A delicious meal is prepared by the chef for the guests.
*   **B.** A delicious meal was prepared by the chef for the guests.
*   **C.** A delicious meal has been prepared by the chef for the guests.
*   **D.** A delicious meal was being prepared by the chef for the guests.

**Correct Answer:** **B**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:**
    *   The active sentence is in the **simple past tense** ("prepared").
    *   The rule for converting simple past tense into passive voice is: **Subject (New) + was/were + past participle (V3) of the verb + by + Object (New)**.
    *   "A delicious meal" is singular, so we use the singular past auxiliary **"was"** followed by the V3 verb **"prepared"**, which gives us: *"A delicious meal was prepared by the chef for the guests."* (Option B).
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **A. A delicious meal is prepared...** incorrectly uses the simple present tense auxiliary "is".
    *   **C. A delicious meal has been prepared...** incorrectly uses the present perfect tense auxiliary "has been".
    *   **D. A delicious meal was being prepared...** incorrectly uses the past continuous auxiliary "was being".

### **Question 30 (Direct/Indirect Speech Conversion)**
**Select the option that represents the correct indirect speech conversion of the given direct sentence:**

**He said to me, "Where are you going?"**

*   **A.** He asked me where I was going.
*   **B.** He asked me where was I going.
*   **C.** He said to me where I was going.
*   **D.** He asked me that where I was going.

**Correct Answer:** **A**

**Detailed Explanation:**
*   **Why the correct option is right:**
    1.  In converting a direct interrogative sentence starting with a question-word ("Where") into indirect speech, the reporting verb **"said to"** is changed to **"asked"**.
    2.  No conjunction like **"that"** or **"if/whether"** is used before the Wh-question word.
    3.  The interrogative word order of direct speech ("are you going" - auxiliary verb + subject + verb) is changed into an assertive word order in indirect speech ("I was going" - subject + auxiliary verb + verb).
    4.  The present continuous tense ("are you going") is backshifted to past continuous tense ("I was going") since the reporting verb is in the past tense ("said").
    5.  Therefore, Option A is grammatically and rules-wise correct.
*   **Why the other choices are wrong:**
    *   **B. He asked me where was I going.** is incorrect because it retains the interrogative word order ("was I going") instead of changing to assertive ("I was going").
    *   **C. He said to me where I was going.** is incorrect because the reporting verb "said to" has not been changed to "asked" for an interrogative sentence.
    *   **D. He asked me that where I was going.** is incorrect because the conjunction "that" should never precede a Wh-question word in indirect speech.
